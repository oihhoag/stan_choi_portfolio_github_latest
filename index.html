CASE STUDY 01
Sea Ray / Product Launch / 2025

More Than a Little Luxurious.

An integrated launch campaign for the all-new Sea Ray SLX 360 Outboard, translated into a disciplined visual system across social, email and web.

Role
Creative concepting
Visual design
Campaign rollout
Brand
Sea Ray
Scope
Social carousel
Instagram story
Knak email

Project
SLX 360 Outboard launch
The challenge

Introduce a flagship product without reducing luxury to a feature list.

The SLX 360 Outboard launch needed to communicate performance, comfort and design in a voice that felt unmistakably Sea Ray. The campaign had to remain elevated while adapting across formats with very different content demands.

Creative direction

A restrained system built around confident contrasts.

Black editorial fields, precise typography and cinematic boating photography create a premium framework. Benefit-led messages such as “Power can have pulse” and “Comfort can be commanding” transform product attributes into concise emotional statements.

System thinking

One launch language, adapted by channel.

Each touchpoint uses the same core ingredients with a channel-specific hierarchy. Social emphasizes pacing and discovery, Stories deliver immediate benefit-led messages, email supports deeper consideration and the web hero establishes the launch at a glance.

CASE STUDY 01
Lowe Boats / 2025

Hidden in
Plain Sight.

A product launch and campaign system designed to modernize Lowe Roughneck without losing the credibility it had earned with hunters and anglers.

Role
Creative Direction
Brand Experience
Visual Design
Team
Brunswick in-house creative team
Scope
Campaign strategy
Launch system
Digital + print
Brand
Lowe Boats
The challenge

Functional, but no longer distinctive.

Lowe had built a strong reputation around reliable, affordable aluminum boats. Yet the existing creative direction felt dated and increasingly disconnected from younger, lifestyle-driven hunters. The opportunity was not to make the brand louder, but to make it feel more relevant and emotionally credible.

Audience insight

Hunters don’t just buy boats. They buy trust.

Competitive analysis showed a category crowded with toughness, tradition and feature claims. Lowe could differentiate by demonstrating that the brand understood the hunter’s mindset, environment and need for equipment that feels dependable rather than performative.

Creative strategy

Blend in to stand apart.

The campaign concept, “Hidden in Plain Sight,” drew from the new Mossy Oak camouflage patterns and reframed camouflage as a symbol of intelligence, precision and belonging. The visual system paired organic camouflage fields with disciplined typography and direct messaging.

  • Earth-toned, swamp-inspired palette
  • Camouflage integrated into layouts and typography
  • Authentic hunting environments over polished studio staging
  • A message centered on stealth, utility and identity
Benchmarking & competitive analysis

Finding a space the category was not already occupying.

I reviewed the visual conventions, messaging patterns and positioning used across competing aluminum hunting and fishing brands. The analysis revealed a category dominated by toughness claims, heritage cues and functional feature language, creating an opportunity for Lowe to feel more contemporary without losing credibility.

View benchmark and competitive analysis
Mood board & visual language

Translating strategy into a distinct visual direction.

The mood board established the campaign’s tone before execution: swamp-inspired color, environmental texture, camouflage integration, restrained typography and imagery that felt authentic to the hunting experience rather than staged.

View mood board and color palette
Outcome

A new direction with room to scale.

The campaign elevated Lowe’s visual perception while retaining its affordability and authenticity. “Hidden in Plain Sight” also established a reusable creative theme for future hunting-focused communication within the Lowe brand family.

CASE STUDY 02
Lowe Boats / 2025

Two letters.
Infinite stories.

Transforming LS from a forgettable alphanumeric product name into an emotionally flexible lifestyle platform.

Role
Creative Direction
Campaign Strategy
Visual Design
Team
Brunswick in-house creative team
Scope
Brand storytelling
Art direction
Launch campaign
Brand
Lowe Boats
The situation

From model code to lifestyle idea.

The value pontoon category was dominated by either dry specification-led messaging or aspirational luxury. LS had the chance to occupy the space between them: stylish, social and attainable. The problem was that the name itself carried no emotional meaning.

Audience insight

People weren’t asking for another pontoon name. They wanted more reasons to get on the water.

For first-time boaters, boating can feel financially and culturally out of reach. The campaign needed to lower that barrier without diminishing the quality or appeal of the product.

Big idea

Make LS mean something.

Instead of avoiding the plainness of the name, the concept turned it into a flexible storytelling device:

  • Long Summers — endless days on the water
  • Life is Sweet — celebrating simple pleasures
  • Limitless Smiles — joy without barriers

This system gave the campaign a recognizable structure while allowing each execution to express a different emotional facet of the product.

Photoshoot art direction

Defining the visual world before the camera rolled.

I developed the photoshoot mood board to establish the lighting, emotional tone, casting moments and product-detail priorities for the LS launch. It became the shared visual reference used to align the production team before the shoot.

The final photography closely followed this direction: warm golden-hour light, natural family and friend interactions, active boating moments and product features captured within real use.

View photoshoot mood board
Video storyboard

Designing the launch as a sequence, not a collection of shots.

I developed the launch-video storyboard to translate the LS campaign idea into a clear filming plan. The sequence moves from warm product introductions and lifestyle moments to feature details, social energy and a final full-boat reveal. Each frame identifies the intended camera perspective, narrative purpose and primary visual focus so the production team could capture a cohesive story rather than disconnected footage.

View full launch storyboard
Launch video

From storyboard to final launch film.

The final video carries the storyboard’s visual rhythm into motion—balancing product detail, social energy and warm lifestyle moments to introduce the LS Pontoon as an attainable but elevated boating experience.

Video editing by Shannon.
Campaign design

Extending the launch idea across campaign touchpoints.

The LS narrative system was translated into a flexible set of campaign executions across static, digital and motion formats. Each piece uses the same core visual language while adapting hierarchy, pacing and messaging to the needs of the channel.

View campaign design examples
Art direction

Scenes that feel like memories, not advertisements.

The photoshoot direction emphasized warm natural light, candid interactions and motion. The goal was to show families and friends sharing believable moments, giving the LS story a human center rather than treating the boat as an isolated product object.

Boston Whaler / Brand refresh + product launches

Protect the legacy.
Move the brand forward.

A multi-phase case study spanning research and early visual exploration, followed by independently led launch creative for the 330 Outrage and 290 Outrage.

01—05 / Selected work
My role across the project

The strongest contribution was not a single concept. It was helping define what needed to change—and then carrying the refreshed system into two major launches.

Brand refreshResearch + early exploration

I contributed heavily to the audit, competitive benchmarking and initial visual directions alongside the creative team.

System developmentTeam-led evolution

Another designer on our team led the later development of the refreshed system. I was not involved in the final guideline production.

Product launchesIndependent creative ownership

After the guidelines were completed, I independently carried the 330 Outrage and 290 Outrage launch creative.

01
Research & strategic framing

Before style, we needed clarity.

The project began because both the client and creative team felt increasingly constrained by an aging visual system. The ask was evolutionary—not a dramatic reinvention: preserve the equity of an established premium brand while making its marketing feel more contemporary, refined and flexible.

Research contribution

I helped turn a broad feeling—“the brand looks dated”—into specific, actionable design opportunities.

01

Visual inconsistency

Recent work lacked a clear hierarchy and repeatable graphic behavior across channels.

02

Premium perception gap

The marketing did not consistently reflect the product quality, reputation or price point.

03

Category sameness

Competitors often relied on familiar blue-water imagery, making distinctiveness difficult.

04

Equity worth protecting

The Boston Whaler name, red accent and iconic W carried meaningful recognition and trust.

Strategic response

Refine rather than replace. Strengthen hierarchy, modernize the palette, establish more flexible graphic treatments and use the W mark as a more prominent, ownable brand asset.

02
Early visual exploration / Team effort

Four ways to evolve without losing recognition.

Four designers explored distinct expressions of the same strategic brief. The concepts are shown at equal scale because the value of this phase came from comparison: each direction tested a different balance of heritage, confidence, warmth and modernity.

Four creative directions

Four distinct directions shown together at equal visual weight.

01 / 04
Concept 01

Hard-Won Confidence

Rugged, proven and unapologetically capable—built around the brand’s unsinkable reputation.

Concept 02

American Standard

A confident, familiar direction drawing on coastal Americana and enduring utility.

Concept 03

Textbook Serenity

A quieter, more refined expression using a sophisticated palette, editorial restraint and a prominent W mark.

Concept 04

Emboldened Ambition

A forward-driving direction combining heritage cues with energetic, contemporary contrast.

Ownership note

I developed one of the four early concepts and contributed to the shared research and strategic framing. Another designer on our creative team later led the evolution of the final system, which became a broader team effort.

University of Illinois / Senior Capstone / 2020

Time to Bus

An academic UI/UX exploration focused on making everyday bus travel feel easier to understand, anticipate and navigate.

Context
Senior capstone
Student project
Discipline
UX research
Information architecture
UI design
Format
Mobile application concept
Status
Academic work
Completed 2020
Project context

My first end-to-end UI/UX case study.

Time to Bus was created during my senior year as a university capstone project. It represents an early exploration of product thinking—moving beyond visual communication to consider user needs, task flow, information hierarchy and interaction.

Design challenge

Reducing uncertainty in a time-sensitive transit experience.

Bus riders often have to interpret schedules, arrival information, routes and walking time while already under pressure. The concept explored how a mobile experience could present the most important information first and help riders make faster, more confident decisions.

UX approach

Organizing the experience around the rider’s immediate question: What should I do next?

The project considered the journey from finding a nearby stop to understanding the next arrival and preparing to leave. The interface direction prioritized clear timing, recognizable routes and a focused hierarchy rather than exposing every transit detail at once.

Academic process

From problem framing to interface concept.

01Frame

Define the commuter problem and the moments of uncertainty.

02Organize

Map information and prioritize the actions needed during a trip.

03Explore

Develop user flows and early interface directions.

04Design

Translate the system into a consistent mobile UI concept.

Selected project archive

Process, interface explorations and final presentation.

Original images from the 2020 senior capstone project, shown in presentation order.

Reflection

An early project that shaped how I approach design systems and user needs today.

The visual language reflects the period in which it was created, but the project remains important as my first structured attempt to connect research, navigation and interface design. In a future iteration, I would validate the core flow with current riders, account for accessibility and service disruptions, and rebuild the interface using a contemporary component system.
